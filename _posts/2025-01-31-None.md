Buses are vehicles for millions of passengers making their journeys every day. Double decker buses are extremely rare in the world, and can only be found in none other than the UK and Hong Kong. Finding a double decker bus parked in a school's yard is perhaps even rarer, but my school PLKCKY was one of the lucky ones granted a retired bus back in 2017.

The bus was named STEM bus, and for 7 years it served as an exhibition for a flourishing collection of science and art projects from CKY's students. One of the exhibits was the Bus Vacant Seat Detector, a project that myself and several others have participated in. To embark on building expansion plans, the school has recently decided to send the bus to the scrapyard for its final retirement. In vew of this, I thought it would be a great time to revisit and journal some of the moments along our journey in relation to the fruition of the STEM bus.

* * *

## Modest beginnings

Back in 2014, David, Lincoln and I embarked on our first project in the "Hong Kong Science Project Competition" (SSPC), an annual event where students can enroll for either invention or reserarch based submissions. We worked on a device called the "Blind Assistance Machine". It was a trolley equipped with a number of ultrasonic and infrared sensors, and provided the user of suggestions of which direction to navigate when there is an obstacle in front of him. It was in our aspiration that the device would be a better alternative over a conventional blind cane, but it was quite unreliable in numerous indoor and outdoor situations, as one would expect from student designed prototypes.

While schoool projects were commonplace for us, participating in inter-school events was a first. So was getting our hands on soldering, programming and a number of other design tasks. And so was Mr Louis Wong, my then sicence teacher who just joined CKY! The outcome of our submission was modest, but this was certainly a year of many firsts.

* * *

## Gaining momentum

It was clear that we worked on a solution too complex for the problem we were trying to solve. Undeterred we joined the competition again with a near-identical bill of materials, but with a very different idea: a bus vacant seat detector. The concept was straightforward - install the sensors on each individual seat and use a map of LEDs indicating the vacancy of all the seats.

With the inconveniences of a double-decker bus in mind, we thought our solution would be a great idea, as it could inform passengers at the lower deck of seats available on the upper deck. This would rid them of the effort to go upstairs only to realise there are no seats left, and would reduce the number of potential accidents too. Here's a video showing the prototype, narrated by Jon Maw, featuring actors Lincoln and Clement Li.

https://youtu.be/TYAg4XBYO3c?si=atZ3nTbSZ5tmjG2x&t=54

In 2015, this idea seemed seemed somewhat novel, as KMB or other bus opeartors in Hong Kong had yet to deploy their own solutions to this problem. Today, the solution deployed by KMB is a motion sensor located at the staircase, which informs passengers of the number of available seats available. It is undeniable that this is a smarter and more robust solution, but more importantly it was satisfying to learn that the operators actually cared about the problem and was actively trying to provide solutions too.

![](/assets/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/DSCN5822-scaled.jpg) ![](/assets/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/IMG_4977-scaled.jpg)

Surprisingly, in the sea of innovative and advanced project we won an award for this. Was it the practicality of the solution? The way we presented its marketability? Or did they just like the idea in general?

* * *

## Ups and downs

Oddly enough there was still some amount of interest. Besdies the additional exhibition at the Hong Kong Science museum, we were also invited to appear on an ATV programme. Admittedly this opportunity was in part due to the competition champion declining to appear on the show, but it was certainly an interesting time in history to appear in an ATV show considering its impending loss of license. Today, the recording is unfortunately not available as the company (specifically its public broadcasting services) is no more.

![](/assets/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/12132494_521765487991060_8309369603192768925_o.jpg) ![](/assets/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/12095028_521765484657727_7663157091985478808_o.jpg)

For some time, we had no idea where this was all headed at. We decided to re-enroll ourselves in the competition with the same hardware, but promote it with an app, apply a fresh coat of paint, and market it as a minibus vacant seat detector. In retrospect this was a misstep and I am not clear why we even bothered. To be fair, we are talking about a student project so there was no expectation this project would be going anywhere anyways.
